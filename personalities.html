What Is Personality?

Personality refers to relatively stable patterns of thinking, feeling, and behaving that make each person distinctive across time and situations.

  • Focuses on patterns, not single events
  • Describes both similarities and differences among people
  • Different theories emphasize different causes of these patterns

Quick Check

No right or wrong answer — just your own understanding.

Sample: Personality is the relatively stable way a person tends to think, feel, and act across different situations over time.
🧠 Psychodynamic Theory

Psychodynamic Theory

Emphasizes unconscious motives, early childhood experiences, and internal conflicts as key influences on personality.

  • Mind structures (id, ego, superego) can be in conflict
  • Defense mechanisms reduce anxiety (e.g., denial, projection)
  • Adult behavior may reflect unresolved childhood conflicts

❤️ Humanistic Theory

Humanistic Approaches

Emphasizes human potential, personal growth, self-concept, and the conditions that help or block people from becoming their authentic selves.

  • Self-concept — how you see and think about yourself
  • Unconditional positive regard — acceptance without conditions
  • Conditional positive regard — acceptance tied to meeting expectations
  • Self-actualization — realizing your fullest potential

Reflection

Self-concept
All the beliefs and evaluations you have about yourself — your sense of who you are.
Unconditional Positive Regard (UPR)
Accepting and supporting a person regardless of what they say or do (Carl Rogers).
Self-actualization
The motivation to realize one's full potential — at the top of Maslow's hierarchy of needs.
Conditional Positive Regard
Acceptance that depends on meeting certain standards or expectations; can distort self-concept.
🌐 Social-Cognitive Theory

Social-Cognitive Theory

Personality arises from the interaction of thoughts, behaviors, and environments — a concept Bandura called reciprocal determinism.

  • Observational learning — learning by watching others
  • Self-efficacy — belief in your own ability to succeed
  • Situational influences — context shapes behavior

Scenario

Case Study Alex often hesitates to speak up in class. Last semester, when Alex answered a question and got it wrong, some classmates laughed. Now Alex thinks, "I'm bad at speaking in class," and usually stays silent.
Part A — Multiple Choice

Which concept best explains how Alex's belief "I'm bad at speaking in class" affects future behavior?

Part B — Short Answer
Example: Alex's negative self-belief (low self-efficacy) leads to silence in class, which prevents corrective experiences; the classroom environment (peer ridicule) reinforced that belief, creating a cycle where thought, behavior, and environment all maintain the pattern.
